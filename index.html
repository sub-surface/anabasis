ANABASIS

Feed the machine a photograph and it forgets the photograph. What survives is only the brightness of each pixel, which the renderer reads as elevation — and from that it hallucinates the one kind of world it knows how to make: a terrain, fogged and memoryless, that was never anywhere.

Anabasis — from Xenophon — means a march up from the coast into the interior; a going-inland, an ascent. It felt right for a tool you enter at the shore of an image and climb into, away from whatever the picture once meant.

The piece is after Joan Fontcuberta's Orogenesis (2002–2006), where he fed paintings by Turner and Cézanne into military terrain software built to read maps. I keep coming back to it because it's the cleanest demonstration that an image is just data a system is free to misread — the medium really is the message. As a documentary photographer that idea unsettles me in a good way: here my own pictures get stripped of their witness and re-grown as landscape that remembers nothing.
